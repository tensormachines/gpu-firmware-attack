Problem Thesis

Narrow accelerator dependence creates a high-leverage supply-chain target. The attack is valuable precisely because it can remain silent.

1. The dependency

An AI product can become coupled to a narrow GPU class because of kernel optimizations, memory behavior, or tooling. This coupling concentrates risk. A compromise of that class affects model integrity, model confidentiality, and product reliability across all deployments that share the same accelerator lineage.

Operational symptom

Competitor velocity increases while your model quality drifts downward without a clear software regression.

2. The attack surface

Firmware and driver layers sit below most application telemetry. A backdoor here can modify kernels, perturb numerics, introduce selective data leakage, or exfiltrate proprietary artifacts. Outputs can remain plausible enough to evade naive tests. The attacker’s advantage is persistence and invisibility.

Why naive checks fail

A compromised stack can behave correctly on known tests while diverging on real workloads.

3. The detection problem

Any verifier that runs entirely inside the compromised boundary is becomes a modifiable code. Detection must rely on signals that can be validated outside the attacker’s control such as Tensor's physics-based verification loop. This is a continuous verification problem.

4. Tensor Machines scope (applied to models)

Tensor Machines addresses this problem through two capabilities only. First, dynamic model watermark verification: a verifier (TEE, measured host, or cloud control plane) issues secret, rotating canary challenges and checks whether responses match the authentic signature distribution. Second, physics-layer baselining: per-device execution fingerprints (power/timing/thermal/EMI) are learned and monitored for drift consistent with tampering, degradation, or adversarial interference.

What is being verified

Tensor Machines does not rely on firmware-resident truth. It verifies the model via secret, rotating canary challenges (dynamic watermark verification) and validates that execution remains consistent with per-device physics baselines. Divergence becomes evidence of tampering or degradation.

Tensor Machines detection loop

The system learns a baseline for each device and workload class, then continuously compares live execution against that baseline. Baselines can include multi-modal side channels such as power draw trajectories, timing distributions, thermal responses, and electromagnetic characteristics. In parallel, an independent verifier issues rotating canary challenges and checks whether model responses match expected signature distributions.

Practical output

A device-level trust score plus hypotheses: benign variability, drift, anomaly, or likely compromise.

What Tensor Machines does not claim

Perfect prevention of exfiltration. The defensible claim is early detection + isolation using independent evidence.

Interpretation

The verifier gains confidence when independent signals agree: physics baselines drift in ways consistent with compromised execution, and canary-response signatures deviate from the authentic model beyond what background variability explains.

Key claim

Verification is external: detection comes from independent evidence, not from the compromised stack describing itself.

What the simulator is modeling

The simulator models a monitoring loop that learns per-device baselines and flags anomalies. Poisoning and exfiltration increase operational impact. Physics baselines provide an execution-level signal that becomes harder to fake as sensing coverage improves. Dynamic watermark verification reduces ambiguity by continuously probing whether the model still behaves like the authentic distribution.

Assumptions

(1) physics baselines exist per device/workload, (2) canary probes remain secret and rotate, (3) the verifier is independent (TEE/measured host/cloud).

Non-goal

This is not a proof that attackers cannot adapt. It shows how evidence accumulation improves detection and reduces dwell time.

Limits and honest boundaries

What remains hard even with strong monitoring. This keeps the story technically credible.

1. Exfiltration is not fully preventable by monitoring

A sufficiently privileged implant can leak data. This approach reduces time-to-detect by observing execution drift and identifying anomalies consistent with unauthorized computation. The defensible claim is early detection and isolation, not perfect prevention.

2. False positives must be controlled

Real hardware varies with temperature, load, power delivery, and scheduling. Physics baselines must be conditioned on context and calibrated per device class. Dynamic canary verification helps separate intentional tampering from benign drift and background variability.

3. Attackers can attempt selective evasion

Attackers may behave correctly on some probes. This is why canaries must be secret and rotating, and why physics evidence matters. The goal is not a single decisive test, but sustained consistency under monitoring.

4. Scope discipline is a strength

Tensor Machines does not need to own the entire stack. It provides independent integrity signals: dynamic model watermark verification and physics-layer drift detection. These signals integrate into existing security programs as a distinct line of evidence.

This single-page narrative is intentionally constrained to Tensor Machines’ two pillars for this problem: dynamic model watermark verification (canary challenges + independent verifier) and physics-layer baselining. The core claim is that these capabilities produce independent evidence of compromise even when the GPU stack is treated as potentially hostile.
